Filing Bankruptcy Pro Se: What You Need to Know

The decision to file bankruptcy without an attorney should be made carefully. While it is your legal right, bankruptcy involves complex rules about exemptions, means testing, reaffirmation agreements, and discharge eligibility that can significantly affect the outcome of your case. Courts provide standardized forms but limited guidance on strategy. If you proceed pro se, take advantage of every free resource available: court self-help desks, law school bankruptcy clinics, legal aid organizations, and the detailed instructions accompanying each official form.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I file bankruptcy without a lawyer?

Yes, you have the constitutional right to file bankruptcy pro se (without an attorney). However, bankruptcy law is complex and errors can result in dismissal, loss of property, or failure to discharge debts. Many courts offer self-help resources, and legal aid organizations may provide free or low-cost assistance.

What are the risks of filing pro se?

Common risks include incorrectly valuing assets, failing to claim all available exemptions, missing deadlines, improperly listing creditors, and making errors on the means test. These mistakes can lead to case dismissal, loss of exempt property, or non-dischargeable debts that could have been discharged with proper counsel.

Where can I get help filing bankruptcy pro se?

Free resources include the court's self-help desk, bankruptcy clinic programs at law schools, legal aid societies, and the court's local rules and forms. The U.S. Courts website (uscourts.gov) provides official forms and instructions. Some nonprofit organizations also offer free bankruptcy preparation assistance.
